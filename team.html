S. Sakuma (D2)

Thesis area: machine learning

I. Hosoya (M1)

Thesis area: data inferences

Sakuma and Hosoya2 are working on generating machine learning and inference models to characterize potential links between gene expressions and phenotypic changes. The ability to correlate gene expressions is partly due to the Center's focus in drug discovery and our development of pyrrole-imidazole polyamides (PIPs), a class of programmable DNA minor-groove binders that have exceptional motif recognition specificity. The ability to synthesize PIPs with differential binding motifs allow us to selectively inhibit or even activate transcription, and these members then use information we obtain from microarrays and NGS experiments to make models that help us understand how the genomic landscape can be altered.

Our Computing Resources

rg20

We have built and operate rg20, a small-scale computing cluster; the namesake is merely a concatenation of rinshogenome ("Clinical Genomics") and the year I began as a staff scientist. rg20 has a hair over 300 x64 threads and 1.6 terabytes of RAM distributed in nodes of Dell, Fujitsu and HP machines linked together by a modified version of Son of Grid scheduler/queuing system. NVIDIA Corporation also provided us with a few ARM64 Jetson Nano SBC's via the Jetson Nano Developer Grant for instructional and research exercises in short-read sequencing analysis.

Another rack in the rg20 cluster.
Some of the Jetson Nano SBC's used in the lab.

Besides research projects outlined here, rg20 also hosts some of the bioinformatics services that others at CCCRI use from time to time.

SHIROKANE

For tasks that require more resources or environments that rg20 cannot sufficiently provide, we rely on the Human Genome Center's SHIROKANE supercomputer system at University of Tokyo Institute of Medical Sciences, especially in situations where GPUs are required.

For Aspiring Degree Seekers

†I said Japanese equivalent because I am actually unsure whether your diploma will actually bear the verbatim titles of Master of Science (M.S.) or Doctor of Phiolosophy (Ph.D.) since Japanese institutions issue degrees in a slight different way than most western universities. While degrees such as 理学博士 (化学) (lit. "doctorate of science in chemistry"), 工学修士 (lit. "master of engineering"), or 医学博士 (lit. "doctorate of medicine") are decidedly different in title, in Japan they are all research-based degrees and do not reflect any sort of professional or terminal post-baccrelaureate training. This is different compared to say the U.S., where a M.Eng and M.S. in engineering can differ in that one is coursework-based and one research-based, or that a M.D.†† holder has undergone medical training and is qualified for medical licensure, in Japan all these degrees are research-based, awarded from different departments upon satisfying their graduation requirements. By "Japanese equivalent" I do not mean in any way that they're not real degrees, merely that the transliteration of diploma titles will not always match word-for-word what is quoted in admission pamphlets in English. This is more like the weirdness of how Harvard gives out AB degrees that are just funny names for BA, but not like how a BA in chemistry at Berkeley differs from a BS in chemistry.
††Medical education in Japan is not post-graduate like the U.S. and technically medical school graduates get awarded a Bachelor in Medicine or Surgery; it is confusing since most physicians call themselves MD's anyway, and if they have gone to graduate school to become a physician scientist they often call themselves MD/PhDs like how most MSTP graduates are in the U.S. Again, this is an intricating consequence of how each country structures their education system.
tl/dr do NOT question your family physician if the diploma he/she hangs on the wall in the office ONLY says a bachelor of medicine! Or I guess you can suspect, it is entirely up to you.
